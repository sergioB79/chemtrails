Atmospheric Physics

What contrails actually are

Contrails are a well-understood atmospheric phenomenon documented since the 1940s, governed by basic thermodynamics, and explained in aviation meteorology textbooks for decades.

🌡️

Formation mechanism

Jet engines produce hot, humid exhaust. At cruising altitude (~35,000 ft), ambient temperatures hover around −50°C. Hot water vapor instantly condenses and freezes into ice crystals. Same physics as seeing your breath on a cold morning — just at 11,000 meters.

⏱️

Why some persist and others don't

Duration depends entirely on atmospheric humidity. In dry air, contrails vanish in seconds. In supersaturated air (RHi > 100%), they persist for hours and spread into cirrus-like sheets. Fully described by the Appleman criterion — no unknowns remain.

🌈

Iridescent "rainbow" contrails

Ice crystal light scattering — identical to the optics of halos and rainbow clouds. The German DLR Atmospheric Physics Department published a full predictive model. Standard optics. No extra chemicals.

✈️

Why there are more trails now

Global air traffic increased roughly 5–6× since the 1970s. More flights means more contrails. Climate change may also be increasing supersaturated atmospheric conditions at altitude. Documented, measurable causes.

🔲

Grid patterns in the sky

The US National Airspace System uses fixed flight lanes in a geometric grid. Aircraft on perpendicular routes at different times, in humid conditions, naturally produce crosshatch patterns. Air traffic control geometry.

🧪

What's actually in jet exhaust

Combustion produces water vapor, CO₂, nitrogen oxides, trace soot, and trace sulfur. The contrail is almost entirely ice. No barium, no aluminum salts, no biological agents — and no delivery mechanism for these exists at scale without global detection.

Thermodynamics — Schmidt 1941 / Appleman 1953

The equation that explains everything

The Schmidt-Appleman criterion is the thermodynamic model that predicts when a contrail forms and whether it persists. Derived in 1941 and refined by Herbert Appleman in 1953. It leaves no room for mystery — and no need for secret chemicals.

Engineering Analysis

The delivery problem — why it doesn't work

Even setting aside all the evidence above, the chemtrail theory fails on basic engineering grounds. High-altitude aircraft are perhaps the worst conceivable delivery mechanism for any substance intended to affect a ground-level population. Here is why, systematically.

01

Atmospheric dispersion — the dilution is catastrophic

Any substance released at cruise altitude (~10,000m) immediately encounters wind shear, turbulent wake diffusion, and continuous tropospheric convection. Using standard Gaussian atmospheric dispersion modelling, a substance released at 10km altitude reaches ground level diluted by a factor of:

10⁸ – 10¹⁰
That is 100 million to 10 billion times diluted before reaching ground level.
At this concentration, virtually any known substance — toxic or otherwise — is completely inert.

To deliver a biologically meaningful dose at ground level you would need to release industrial quantities continuously — quantities detectable by any atmospheric chemistry monitoring station on Earth, of which there are hundreds operated by independent agencies worldwide.

02

Particle size physics — payload vs. suspension are mutually exclusive

For a particle to stay aloft long enough to drift to ground level, it must be extremely fine. But fine particles carry negligible chemical payload. The two requirements are physically incompatible at scale:
